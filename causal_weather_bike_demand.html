In [4]:
helpers.plot_usage_per_temperature(hourly, metric='total')

Interpretation: extreme cold temperatures (0-20% percentiles) influence demand the most

For an hour to be considered 'bad weather' it needs to satisfy one of:

  1. Light or Heavy falls (rain, snow, hailing); or
  2. Extreme cold temperatures (0-20% percentiles)

Naive effect estimate¶

In [6]:
helpers.plot_naive_estimate(hourly, metric='total')

Interpretation: bad weather reduces demand by ~60% (naive estimation)

  • This estimate is biased because weather is not randomly assigned
  • We should compare demand under different weather conditions within the same time context (hour, weekday, season)
  • That would isolate the causal effect of weather as the naïve method overestimates the effect

Causal Estimate of Weather Effect¶

  • Method: regression model with time fixed effects to control for confounding factors
  • Causal Estimate: the causal effect of bad weather on bike demand is estimated at ~40% (-39% ± 2% at 95% confidence)
  • This compares demand under different weather conditions within the same hour of day, month, and year
  • As expected, the naive estimate indeed overestimates the effect

Ensuring the causal model is robust¶

  • The estimated causal effect rapidly converges with model complexity to estimated ~40%
  • The effect stabilizes once time confounders are controlled
  • We ensured causal model is robust, so we can have high confidence in the final estimate

Segmented Analysis by User Type¶

  • Bad weather reduces demand by ~39% overall
  • By user type:
    • casual users: −51% (highly elastic)
    • registered users: −36% (more inelastic)
    • difference: ~15% between casual and registered
  • Casual usage is more discretionary while registered usage is more driven by necessity

Effect depends on hour of day¶

Demand sensitivity varies significantly by time:

  • Most resilient: early morning (−29%)
  • Most elastic: off-peak (−46%), afternoon commute (−48%)
  • About 20% difference between most resilient and most elastic

Limitations¶

  1. Unobserved Demand Shocks (concert, public transport disruptions, ...):
    • Events may affect demand independently of weather
  2. Geo-localization:
    • Weather and demand vary accross location
    • Data and model are aggregated and would overlook location-based effects
  3. Simplified Weather definition:
    • We defined bad weather as a binary variable, it doesn't capture the intensitiy or coupling effects of weather

Potential Improvements¶

  • Incorporate external event and transport data
  • Use location-level modeling
  • Model weather as continuous and add interactions

Key Takeaways¶

  • Bad weather reduces demand by ~39% (not 60% as naive method suggested)
  • Effects are heterogeneous (user type and time of day)
  • Targeted interventions would outperform uniform policies
